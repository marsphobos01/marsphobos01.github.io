Building Resleeve: turning albums into wallpapers

Resleeve started with a small, slightly petty frustration: I wanted a clean, poster-style wallpaper of an album I'd been listening to on repeat, and everything I could find online was either low-resolution, plastered in watermarks, or just ugly. So I did the developer thing and decided it'd be quicker to build it myself. (It was not quicker. It never is.)

What it does

You type in an artist or album, and Resleeve pulls the metadata — cover art, full tracklist, total runtime, release year — and composes it into a balanced, downloadable wallpaper. The goal was for the output to look designed, not auto-generated: considered spacing, a colour palette pulled from the artwork itself, and typography that doesn't fight the cover.

How it's put together

The backend is Python with Flask handling the routes and a rendering layer that builds the final image. The flow is roughly:

  • Look up the album and fetch its metadata and high-resolution artwork.
  • Sample the cover to build a small, harmonious colour palette for the layout.
  • Lay out the artwork, tracklist and details on a canvas with consistent margins and type scale.
  • Export it at wallpaper resolution so it actually looks sharp on a real screen.

The bit that was actually hard

Honestly? The design, not the code. Pulling data is straightforward; making the result look good across every album — a minimalist white cover and a chaotic neon one — is the hard part. A layout that's gorgeous for one record falls apart for the next. Most of my time went into rules that hold up regardless of the input: how the palette adapts, how long tracklists wrap, how the type scales when an album has 27 songs versus 8.

The lesson that keeps repeating for me: the engineering is rarely the hard part — the judgement is.

What's next

A few things I want to add: more layout templates, light and dark variants, and better handling for deluxe editions with enormous tracklists. If you've got an album you think would look great as a poster, tell me — testing it against real edge cases is half the fun.

You can try the current version at resleeve.marsphobos.com.
